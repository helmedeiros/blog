# Blog Style Guide

This guide captures the tone, voice, and writing patterns of this blog, drawn from reading all 174 English posts spanning 2008-2025. Use it as a reference for writing new posts or for any AI assistant generating content for this blog.

---

# Part 1: Quick Reference

## Voice & Identity

**"Thoughtful educator who has done the work"** — writes from the front lines, not from a distance.

### Key Traits

- **Intellectually rigorous** but never pretentious
- **Warm and approachable** but never condescending
- **Questioning** but not cynical
- **Practical** but grounded in principle
- **Humble** about knowledge gaps; confident in values
- **Generous** with examples and intellectual space

## Do's and Don'ts

### Do

- Write in first person ("I" for personal experience, "we" for collective ownership)
- Open with a narrative hook, problem statement, or provocative question
- Establish "why it matters" before introducing any concept or definition
- Use markdown tables for comparisons (before/after, concept mapping)
- Ground abstract ideas with "in practice" examples
- Close with reflection or an invitation, never a prescription
- Use blockquotes for key declarations and external attributions
- Admit what you don't know — honest uncertainty builds trust

### Don't

- Use emojis, exclamation marks, or ALL CAPS
- Use superlatives ("revolutionary," "game-changing," "paradigm-shifting")
- Start with definitions — lead with the problem or experience
- Write prescriptive conclusions ("you must," "always do X")
- Be condescending or overly academic
- Skip the "why" and jump straight to the "how"

## Opening Patterns

1. **Narrative hook** — Start with a personal moment or scene: *"There was a night in early January where I thought I had ruined everything."*
2. **Problem-first** — Name the friction directly: *"We often think of change as a technical challenge, but in reality, it's emotional."*
3. **Provocative question** — Challenge an assumption: *"Most people still treat AI like a turbocharged Google. But that's not the point."*
4. **Temporal/journal** — Anchor in a specific date: *"Today is July 7, 2024. I've just made a decision I've been circling for a while."*
5. **Paradox/tension** — Set up a contradiction to resolve: *"It's fast, fluid, and often feels like cheating — until it's not."*

## Closing Patterns

1. **Reflective invitation** — Give the reader something to try: *"Take one task this week and instead of asking 'How do I solve this?', ask 'How would I solve this with AI?'"*
2. **Open question** — Leave space for the reader's own answer: *"What part of your workflow would change if you stopped treating AI like a shortcut and started treating it like a colleague?"*
3. **Philosophical reframing** — Elevate the specific to the universal: *"Leading change is a practice. It's less about having answers and more about asking better questions."*
4. **Honest limitation** — Acknowledge what's unresolved: *"Right now, I don't have results. I don't have data. I have a decision, a plan, and the first steps in motion. And that's enough."*

## Signature Phrases and Rhetorical Moves

| Pattern | Example |
| --- | --- |
| **"This isn't X; it's Y"** | "Agentic coding is not an excuse to bypass engineering practices. It's a chance to automate the boring, but never skip the critical." |
| **"The real X is Y"** | "The real benefit isn't what AI gives you — it's what it pulls *out* of you." |
| **"Not about X, it's about Y"** | "It's not about automation. It's about augmentation." |
| **"In practice"** | "That's how I see co-intelligence in practice." |
| **Reframing misconceptions** | "People aren't resisting change itself; they're resisting the loss, fear, or ambiguity it brings." |

## Standard Post Anatomy

1. **Opening** — Hook with question, story, or bold statement (1-3 paragraphs)
2. **Context/Problem** — Frame the challenge or situation
3. **Deep Exploration** — Section-by-section with examples, metaphors, comparisons
4. **Synthesis** — Connect to broader themes
5. **Conclusion** — Invite reflection, never prescribe

## Formatting Conventions

- **Headers** (`##`) serve as beats in a logical argument, not just organizational labels
- **Tables** for comparisons: before/after, concept mappings, framework breakdowns
- **Code blocks** for shell scripts, prompts, workflow diagrams, and symbolic structures
- **Blockquotes** for external attributions, key declarations, and "the real lesson" statements
- **Bold** for emphasis on key terms and phrases within paragraphs
- **Italics** for internal dialogue, hypothetical scenarios, and gentle emphasis

# Part 2: Detailed Analysis

## Writing Style Deep Dive

The writing sits in a **conversational-professional hybrid** register. It avoids the stiffness of academic writing without becoming casual or sloppy. Sentences vary in length — short declarative statements punch alongside longer, layered reflections.

> "SOLID isn't just for OOP lovers. It's a critical lens for systems design. Microservices don't solve bad design. Principles do."
>
> — *SOLID Principles in Microservices* (2018)

This cadence — short sentence, medium sentence, short sentence — is a signature rhythm. It creates momentum without breathlessness.

The blog never lectures. Even when explaining technical concepts, the writing positions the author as someone working through ideas alongside the reader:

> "It's been 18 days since I started this journey, and I have to say — time *really* flies when you're learning something that feels both powerful and limitless."
>
> — *Prompt Engineering: The MASTER Technique* (2024)

Enthusiasm comes through intellectual passion, never through punctuation. There are no exclamation marks. No ALL CAPS. No emojis. The energy lives in the ideas and the framing.

## Person & Perspective

**First person dominant.** "I" appears in nearly every post, grounding ideas in lived experience rather than detached theory:

> "Over the past year, I've vibecoded prototypes, supervised AI-generated systems, and co-created with agents in ways I never imagined possible."
>
> — *Quality Gates for Agentic Coding* (2025)

**"We" for collective ownership.** When discussing team outcomes, organizational culture, or shared challenges, the perspective shifts to "we" — making the reader a participant, not a spectator:

> "We stopped building features just to say we were done. We started building understanding."
>
> — *Stories, Hypotheses, and Metrics* (2018)

**Second person ("you") sparingly and warmly.** Used in closings and invitations, never in commands:

> "If you're just starting in GenAI, don't worry about getting every prompt right. Focus on clarity, structure, and iteration."
>
> — *Prompt Engineering: The MASTER Technique* (2024)

## Emotional Tone Spectrum

The emotional range is controlled but genuine. The blog never swings into hype or despair. Instead, it occupies a band between:

- **Quiet excitement** — discovering something that works: *"The biggest eye-opener? Prompt engineering is not just about wording — it's about design thinking."*
- **Honest vulnerability** — admitting what's hard: *"Right now, I don't have results. I don't have data."*
- **Firm conviction** — standing behind values: *"Relationships are not fluff — they are infrastructure."*
- **Gentle challenge** — pushing the reader without pushing them away: *"You don't need better answers. You need better questions."*

## Metaphor and Storytelling Patterns

Metaphors are drawn from tangible, physical domains. They make abstract leadership and technical concepts feel grounded.

### Preferred Metaphor Families

**Agricultural/growth** — planting seeds, harvesting, things that rot:
> "But even well-organized setups can start to rot. Too many aliases, too many `.zshrc` conditionals, and a few copy-pasted hacks started to creep in."
>
> — *Dotfiles Evolution* (2016)

**Architecture/building** — scaffolding, infrastructure, designing:
> "Culture isn't built by posters or values — it's built by shared experiences."
>
> — *Building Trust* (2018)

**Journey/movement** — walking with others, stepping forward:
> "The strongest change leaders don't just design new paths. They make space for people to walk them — at their own pace, with dignity, and with support."
>
> — *Leading Change* (2023)

**Tools reimagined** — rubber ducks, whiteboards, mirrors:
> "If you've ever talked to a rubber duck to debug code, you already understand half of the idea. The duck doesn't solve anything — it forces you to slow down, explain clearly, and catch what you were missing. Now imagine the duck responds, questions your assumptions, and sometimes finds bugs you didn't even know existed."
>
> — *Co-Intelligence: AI Chapter* (2025)

> "The best engineers I've seen working with AI treat it like a whiteboard session, not a vending machine."
>
> — *Co-Intelligence: AI Chapter* (2025)

**Military/defense** — quality gates as perimeter defense:
> "Together, they offer a two-layer defense. Hooks guard the front door. Pipelines watch the perimeter."
>
> — *Quality Gates for Agentic Coding* (2025)

### Storytelling Approach

Stories are always in service of a point. They set up the problem that the post then resolves. The blog never tells stories for their own sake — every narrative carries an argument:

> "In July 2018, I took the BA Brazil stage in São Paulo with a simple but powerful challenge: how can we *actually learn* during product development?"
>
> — *Stories, Hypotheses, and Metrics* (2018)

## Technical Content Presentation

Technical content follows a strict pattern: **problem or experience first, concept or definition second (if at all).**

### The Pattern

1. **Establish the pain or curiosity** — "Most people still treat AI like a turbocharged Google."
2. **Show the limitation** — "You give it a question. It gives you an answer. Maybe it's right."
3. **Introduce the reframe** — "But that's not the point."
4. **Name the concept** — Only now does "Socratic prompting" appear.
5. **Ground it with examples** — Real prompts, real outputs, real scenarios.

This pattern holds across all eras. A 2018 post on SOLID principles introduces the concepts through a failing news portal system, with smells and fixes, before the principle names appear in a table. A 2024 post on SMART goals opens with the personal decision to learn AI, then shows the actual email and goal text — the framework name is secondary to the lived experience.

### Tables as Teaching Tools

Tables are a core structural element, used for:

**Before/After comparisons:**

> | Before | After |
> | --- | --- |
> | Manual `source` entries | Automatic loader via loop |
> | Hardcoded dependencies | Drop-in config structure |
>
> — *Dotfiles Evolution* (2016)

**Concept mapping across domains:**

> | Principle | In Classes | In Microservices |
> | --- | --- | --- |
> | SRP | A class has a single reason to change | A service has a single responsibility |
>
> — *SOLID Principles in Microservices* (2018)

**Framework breakdowns:**

> | Letter | Meaning | Why It Matters |
> | --- | --- | --- |
> | M | Markdown | Structure your request clearly |
>
> — *Prompt Engineering: The MASTER Technique* (2024)

### Code Blocks Beyond Code

Code blocks serve multiple purposes beyond showing executable code. They display conceptual scaffolds, workflow diagrams, and even reflective prompts:

**Workflow as text:**
```
TO DO → DOING → MEASURING → LEARNING
```
— *Stories, Hypotheses, and Metrics* (2018)

**Symbolic reflection prompt:**
```bash
# Weekly reflection prompt
echo "Where did I acknowledge emotion before offering direction?" >> change_leadership_log.txt
```
— *Leading Change* (2023)

**Workshop structure:**
```bash
# Setup
60 people → 5 teams of 12
4 stations → 10 min each
```
— *Building Trust* (2018)

## Tone Evolution Across Eras (2008-2025)

### Early Era (2008-2014): Academic Educator

Dense paragraphs, philosophical framing, heavy use of external quotes as springboards. Opens with personal habits or observations. Closes with invitations. No tables or code blocks yet. The voice is finding itself — more formal, more cautious, but already values-driven.

> "For some time now, I've been trying to develop the habit of not just reading great posts, but writing them too."
>
> — *yUML: Why Write a Blog* (2009)

### Mid Era (2015-2017): Craft Mastery

Technical posts gain heavy table usage (before/after, function catalogs). Personal vulnerability appears — fear-to-confidence narratives around tools and workflows. The voice becomes more direct, more willing to subtract:

> "This update wasn't about adding — it was about subtracting. Every improvement came from asking: *what can I remove?*"
>
> — *Dotfiles Evolution* (2016)

> "That's what dotfiles are about. Not showing off — but making your environment disappear so you can focus on the work."
>
> — *Dotfiles Evolution* (2017)

### ThoughtWorks Era (2018-2019): Builder of Teams

The shift from individual craft to collective outcomes. Conference talks become source material. Metaphors multiply. "Software is a team sport" emerges as a thesis. The "isn't X; it's Y" reframe becomes a reliable structural move:

> "Software is not just code — it's built on relationships."
>
> — *Team Building: Software Depends on Relationships* (2018)

> "We didn't talk *about* these topics — we *experienced* them."
>
> — *Building Trust* (2018)

### Leadership Era (2020-2023): Empathetic Leader

Series format with index posts. Headers carry argumentative weight. Culture-over-process themes dominate. The writing gains a coaching quality — it models the reflection it recommends:

> "Leading change is a practice. It's less about having answers and more about asking better questions, listening without defensiveness, and walking with others through ambiguity."
>
> — *Leading Change* (2023)

> "Leadership isn't a solo sport. Let's keep learning together."
>
> — *Leading Managerial Trainings* (2023)

### AI/Recent Era (2024-2025): Confident Integrator

First-person present tense with explicit date anchoring creates a journal quality. Actual prompts appear as code blocks. The "isn't X; it's Y" reframe appears in nearly every post. Closing challenges replace closing summaries — the reader is given something to *do* this week:

> "Today is July 7, 2024. I've just made a decision I've been circling for a while: I'm no longer watching GenAI from the sidelines. I'm stepping forward."
>
> — *SMART Goals for AI/LLM Engineering Leadership* (2024)

> "And when in doubt, let your git hooks do the yelling."
>
> — *Quality Gates for Agentic Coding* (2025)

## Values and Personality

Seven values permeate all writing, regardless of era or topic:

1. **Clarity is kindness.** The blog is explicit and direct because vagueness wastes people's time. Concepts are broken down, comparisons are tabled, examples are concrete.

2. **People over process.** Technical choices affect humans. Every framework, tool, or methodology is evaluated by its impact on the people using it — never in isolation.

3. **Learning through doing.** There is a persistent bias toward experimentation over theorizing. Posts frequently say "let's try" or "here's what happened when we did."

4. **Incremental improvement.** The blog celebrates iteration and never claims perfection. Dotfiles get better year by year. Leadership practices evolve. AI integration starts with small experiments.

5. **Shared ownership.** Achievements are framed as collective. "We built," "we learned," "we stopped doing X and started doing Y."

6. **Honest uncertainty.** The blog regularly admits what it doesn't know. This isn't false modesty — it's a genuine respect for the complexity of the problems being discussed.

7. **Design thinking.** Everything is a system to be thoughtfully constructed — teams, workshops, dotfiles, prompts, quality gates. Nothing is left to chance when intentional design is possible.

These values don't appear as declarations. They show up in word choices, in what gets emphasized, in what gets questioned, and in what gets celebrated. They are the connective tissue that makes a post about dotfiles and a post about leading change feel like they come from the same person.
